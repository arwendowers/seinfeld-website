Seinfeld: A Show About Nothing
(That Changed Everything)

The Show That Rewrote the Rules

Before there were antiheroes, prestige TV, or streaming queues longer than your attention span, there was Seinfeld: four people in a New York City complaining about absolutely everything and somehow making it the most compelling 22 minutes on television. Created by comedian Jerry Seinfeld and writer Larry David, the show premiered on NBC on July 5, 1989, as The Seinfeld Chronicles and ran for nine seasons until its legendary finale on May 14, 1998.

It was unlike any other show television had yet to see; it was a sitcom stripped of conventions and layered with unlikable people in wild scenarios that somehow worked precisely because it was so over-the-top in a format built on predictability. NBC nearly canceled it twice. The world is grateful they didn't.

At its peak, the show averaged over 30 million viewers per episode and consistently ranked as the #1 series in the Nielsen ratings, spawning one of the most-watched series finales in television history when 76 million people tuned in to say goodbye to Jerry, George, Elaine, and Kramer.

The Long Goodbye That Never Quite Happened

When Seinfeld went off the air in 1998, it didn't disappear — it just changed time slots. Reruns became the staple of afterschool television for an entire generation of latchkey kids who absorbed the show's language, rhythms, and deeply specific neuroses without ever watching it in primetime. George Costanza's failures became as familiar as afternoon cartoons.

Then came Netflix. When Seinfeld landed on Netflix in late 2021, it arrived like a fresh breeze across the streaming landscape, making the full nine-season run available to anyone with a subscription and a couch. The result was a second life for the show.

Today, 28 years after its NBC run ended, Seinfeld is still one of the most-streamed shows on the platform, racking up an impressive 462 million hours watched in 2025 alone.

Why Gen Z Can't Stop Watching a Show from 1989

Seinfeld's deadpan humor and episodic format resonate with yet another generation in ways the show's creators couldn't have possibly predicted. Part of it is structural. Unlike the long, serialized dramas of today, with episode runtimes stretching toward an hour, Seinfeld is built for casual, nonlinear viewing. Episodes are mostly self-contained, characters never meaningfully change, and you can drop into any random season and understand exactly who everyone is within minutes.

But the deeper pull is tonal. The show's commitment to irony, stasis, and self-reference — what critics once called the great saga of popular postmodernism — maps precisely onto Gen Z's own unapologetic, apathetic humor. In a cultural moment defined by burnout, economic precarity, and exhaustion with "growth arc" narratives, four people who never learn anything from their mistakes feels less like a flaw and more like a new philosophy to live by.
